# HemoScope Severity Classifier — Full Label Audit Findings

This document summarizes the findings of the audit performed in `notebooks/06_full_label_audit.ipynb`, which independently reproduced notebook 04's training dataset and cross-validation methodology, then used the resulting out-of-fold predictions to re-examine every one of the 153 ClinVar-derived training variants and all 10 currently-deployed variants in `backend/data/variants.py`. Every number below is traceable to a printed output earlier in that notebook.

## Honest cross-validated performance

The audit reproduced notebook 04's 5-fold `StratifiedKFold` cross-validation but kept the actual out-of-fold predictions and probabilities for all 153 variants rather than only the per-fold aggregate scores. Scoring those out-of-fold predictions in one pooled pass gives a macro-F1 of 0.502 and an accuracy of 0.654. This is consistent with the originally recorded 5-fold mean of 0.4995 ± 0.1624 macro-F1 (and 0.6533 mean accuracy) stored in `notebooks/models/cv_scores.json` — the two aggregation methods land within a few thousandths of each other, well inside one recorded standard deviation. These pooled and mean-of-folds numbers are the trustworthy generalization estimates for this classifier. They are distinct from, and should not be confused with, the optimistic 1.00 precision/recall/F1 that notebook 04 also reports after refitting the calibrated model on the full 153-row dataset with no held-out split — that figure measures how well the model memorized its own training data, not how it performs on variants it has not seen.

## The severity-flip pattern is concentrated in HBA1, not explained by data quality

Of the 153 variants, 8 (5.2%) have an out-of-fold predicted label that is the opposite extreme of their true label — benign predicted as severe, or severe predicted as benign. These 8 cases account for 15.1% of all 53 misclassifications in the dataset. Every one of the 8 flips occurs in HBA1: 8 of HBA1's 99 rows (8.1%) are flips, while HBB has zero flips among its 54 rows (0.0%). This concentration cannot be explained by HBA1 being a smaller or noisier subset of the data: HBA1 has nearly twice as many training rows as HBB (99 vs 54) and a higher per-gene macro-F1 (0.495 vs 0.402), and its AlphaFold2 pLDDT confidence values are, if anything, more stable than HBB's (mean 98.39 with a standard deviation of 0.52, versus HBB's mean 97.44 with a standard deviation of 2.84). Examining the raw feature values for the 8 flip cases against the feature means and medians of correctly-classified benign and severe HBA1 variants, the flips consistently sit numerically between the two correctly-classified groups rather than at an extreme on either side — they read as genuine boundary cases that the model's decision surface mishandles, not as statistical outliers or AF2 structural-confidence artifacts. A plausible but unconfirmed hypothesis consistent with these numbers is that some alpha-globin pathogenicity routes act through protein stability or degradation mechanisms that none of the 9 training features (sequence conservation, BLOSUM62, PSSM, position entropy, heme distance, burial, interface status, AF2 confidence, contact count) are designed to capture. This is a hypothesis to be tested, not a proven mechanism — the dataset contains no feature that directly measures protein stability or degradation, so it cannot be confirmed from the data already on hand.

## A genuine data leakage bug affects two of the ten deployed variants

Cross-referencing the 10 variants currently shown in the deployed app against the 153-row training set by gene, position, and amino acid codes revealed that two of them — HBB-Glu26Lys (Hemoglobin E) and HBA1-Asp47His (Hemoglobin Hasharon) — were themselves rows in the training data used to fit the classifier. Notebook 05 scores the deployed variants by loading `hemoscope_rf_calibrated.pkl`, the exact model object that notebook 04 fits on all 153 rows with no held-out split, and calling its `predict_proba` directly. For these two variants, that call is an in-sample evaluation of a model on a point it was trained on, not a genuine held-out prediction. For Hemoglobin E specifically, there are three distinct numbers that matter: its ClinVar-derived training label is severe, originating from a raw ClinVar significance string of bare "pathogenic" with no captured zygosity or co-inheritance context; the leaked, in-sample full-fit prediction originally shown in the deployed app was also severe, at 0.6941 confidence; and this audit's honest out-of-fold prediction — produced by a fold of the model that never saw HbE's own label during training — is mild, at 0.905 confidence. The out-of-fold prediction aligns with real clinical consensus that heterozygous HbE alone is a mild condition, while the originally-displayed in-sample value does not. This means the explanation originally written into the deployed app's summary text, that "structural features fooled the model" into overestimating HbE's severity, is not the correct explanation. The correct explanation is a combination of a methodological leakage bug in how the deployed variant was scored, and ClinVar's bare "pathogenic" significance call not capturing the zygosity- and condition-dependent nuance that makes HbE's real-world severity context-dependent. Hemoglobin Hasharon's leakage, by contrast, does not change any conclusion: both the leaked full-fit prediction (mild, 0.847 confidence) and the honest out-of-fold prediction (mild, 0.490 confidence) agree on the predicted label, so the leakage here only inflated the displayed confidence number, not the displayed severity.

## The "mild" class is definitionally ClinVar's uncertain-significance bucket

Cross-tabulating the raw `clinvar_significance` strings against the mapped `severity_label` for all 153 training variants shows a clean many-to-one mapping consistent with the documented `SIGNIFICANCE_MAP` in notebook 01, with one striking feature: all 106 of the 153 variants labeled "mild" — without a single exception — have a raw `clinvar_significance` of "uncertain significance." There is no variant in the "mild" class for any other reason. This means the dataset's "mild" category is not a confirmed clinically-mild phenotype category; it is ClinVar's ambiguous, unresolved classification bucket relabeled as a severity tier. Cross-referencing this against the 8 severity-flip cases shows that none of them are VUS-derived: all 8 carry confident raw ClinVar calls of "likely benign," "likely pathogenic," or "pathogenic/likely pathogenic." The flip errors are therefore errors against solid, confidently-labeled ground truth, not an artifact of soft or ambiguous labels. But the VUS-only composition of the "mild" class is a separate and fundamental limitation of the three-class framing itself, one that should be disclosed regardless of how well or poorly the classifier performs on it.

## Taken together

These four findings mean that the audit's starting point — a single known mislabeled pair, HbS predicted mild and HbE predicted severe — substantially undersold the actual picture. The real picture includes a broader, gene-specific error pattern concentrated entirely in HBA1 that is not explained by sample size, class balance, or structural-model confidence; a methodological data leakage bug that affected the displayed severity or confidence for two of the ten variants shown in the deployed app, including the original published explanation for HbE's mislabeling being itself incorrect; and a structural ambiguity in what the "mild" class represents, since it is composed entirely of ClinVar's uncertain-significance calls rather than any confirmed mild-phenotype determination. Any account of this classifier's reliability — for the deployed app or for a research paper — needs to report all four of these findings together, not the single mislabeled pair in isolation.
